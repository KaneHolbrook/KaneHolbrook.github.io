Nuclear Spin, Magnets and Coffee

On the surface, coffee may not seem to have much to do with nuclear spin and magnets. But leave
it to science to bring seemingly disparate things together. Remember flubber? In a similar vein (with
less Hollywood and more University of Tokyo) an excellent study was conducted using
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) to observe the roasting process of coffee. The study monitored
30 NMR- visible components, tracking them during the roasting process. Tracking coffee this way is
not super easy. Signal processing is done to identify particular components in the coffee and then
multivariate statistical analysis is used to model the behavior of these components.

Many of the findings from this study corroborated other research where Gas Chromatography or Mass
Spectrometry was used for observation. For example, the Univervisty of Tokyo researchers confirmed
that sucrose and chlorogenic acid degrade into quinic acids and polysaccharides. But the beauty of
this study is that it is a very in-depth look at the changes of the chemical components of coffee throughout the roast.

In terms of the type of coffee used in this analysis, all we know is that they were using Arabica coffee
from Columbia. The coffee was roasted with a Hearthware home coffee roaster although the exact
model is not named. Likely it is Hearthware I-Roast that came onto to the market around 2004. It is
a fluid bed roaster and according to Tom at Sweet Maria’s it behaves much like a popcorn popper and
looks like this bad boy.

The coffee was roasted at 428 degrees farenheit (220 Celsius).

One of the most valuable parts of this study was a group of graphs that were created based on the
results. You find the integral value for the signal of a particular component on the y-axis with time of
the roast on the x-axis. This juxtaposition of component signatures gives us a great visualization for
understanding what the individual components are doing during the roasting process.

Lets dig into the results:

Graph A- Chlorogenic Acids

5-CQA, 4-CQA and 3-CQA are types of chlorogenic acid. It has been posited that there are over 30
different types of chlorogenic acid in coffee but the NMR only showed 3 types. The researchers
commented that this is due to the more abundant presence of these three types and that the other 27
or so consist of more trace amounts. In coffee chemistry threshold values are very important, especially
when trying to sort your way through a thousand or more individual volatile compounds. Note the
very steady decrease of 5-CQA to roughly the 2 minute mark until 9 minutes where it looks like it is
completely degraded. Similar activity seems to be happening with the other chlorogenic acids with
3-CQA having a similar negative slope as 5-CQA. The chlorogenic acids in coffee are some of its most
powerful antioxidants but do not add anything pleasant to the cupping table. In fact, if a coffee still
has high amounts of chlorogenic acid after roasting, there will be considerable bitter and astringent
qualities.

Graph B – Quinic Acids

One immediate observation is that the quinic acids tend to increase in signature as the chlorogenic acids
degrade, which fits with chemical observation of chlorogenic acid breaking down during the roasting
process into caffeic acid and quinic acid. This also comports with Flament in his Coffee Flavor Chemistry
when he states, “ In spite of the degradation of quinic acid, he (speaking of a scientist named Blanc from
a study in 1979) observed a relatively good linearity between the disappearance of chlorogenic acid and
the formation of quinic acid during roasting.” If the roasting process continued past what we have in
our current study we would begin to see the quinic acid start to degrade creating what would appear to
be an optimal roast level for certain types of quinic acids at peak levels.

Graph C – Sucrose

Sucrose takes a sharp nosedive during the roasting process and has completely degraded by around the
four minute mark. It has been observed by Baltes and Bachmann that sucrose degrades into phenols
and aliphatic compounds. Others have called sucrose the aroma precursor in coffee because of all of
the compounds it generates that contribute to the overall odor and taste of the finished product.

Graph D – The amino acids

Glutamic acid, alanine,asparagine and GABA are all completely degraded out by 4 minutes of roasting.
This is significant because amino acids make up proteins, which are necessary for the Maillard reaction
that coffee undergoes during roasting, which would kick off around the 4 minute mark on this roaster.
But digging into the Maillard reaction will be for another time.

Graph E- Trigonelline, N-methylpyridinium, Nicotinic acid

Trigonelline gets a lot of chatter in the coffee communities because we have known for a long time
that the breakdown of trigonelline creates some potentially desirable and undesirable final cup
characteristics. Trigonelline undergoes pyrolysis (degradation at high temperatures) and the byproduct
is many different compounds tasting anywhere from dry rotting wood to caramel to tobacco. N-
methylpyridinium and nicotinic acid are products of the pyrolysis of trigonelline so it makes sense that
they would slightly uptick as trigonelline decreases. They also have significance in the odor and taste of
the finished product.

Graph F – Citric Acid, Malic Acid, Lactic Acid, Acetic Acid and Formic Acid

This is a bit of a mixed group so we will unpack it a little. Citric Acid as most of you will recall, is found
in citrus fruit (oranges, lemons, limes, grapefruits). It is often used as a preservative and to add a sour
taste to certain food items. Citric Acid contributes highly to the perceived taste of acid in the final cup of
coffee. The presence of citric acid starts in green coffee as very prominent and around the four minute
mark we see it start to slowly make a negative linear descent. Then it appears to hold steady around 6
and a half minutes, having a much less dramatic slope downward through the end of the roast.

Malic acid, first isolated from apple juice is actually named from a derivative of the Latin word for apple.
It is known for contributing a tart or sour taste to foods and can be clearly tasted in rhubarb. It appears
to have a similar profile during the roast as citric acid, but in lower concentrations.

Lactic acid is mostly present in fermented milk products like yogurt, kefir, etc. It is also implicated with
giving sourdough bread its “sour” aroma and flavor. The presence of lactic acid in unroasted coffee
appears to be very low but throughout the roasting process looks to increase slightly and approach a
limit around the 6 to 6 and a half minute mark.

Acetic acid is next in the chart and Flament writes that “the odor is pungent, stinging sour, unpleasant
when concentrated, less repulsive below 15% in water (Arctander,1967)”. It has a very interesting
behavior as it ramps up and peaks around the 4 minute mark and holds very steady throughout the rest
of the roasting process. It does appear to slowly be degrading from minutes 5-9 and likely continues
that trajectory if roasted into later stages.

Formic acid has a very similar profile shape to acetic acid but peaks higher and descends faster. It has
been implicated with pungency in odor. It is also found in ant venom.

In his Coffee Flavor Chemistry, Flament, commenting on a study done in the late eighties, writes “Maier
calculated the sensory effective part of acids in several samples of Robusta and Arabica commercial
blends and coffee extracts. In all groups, acetic, citric and malic acid were the most important for the
acid taste. It is astonishing that quinic, formic and chlorogenic acids, present in relatively large amounts,
do not contribute much to the acid taste.”

Graph G – The Polysaccharides

Polysaccharides in coffee are mostly present in the cellular structure of the coffee seed though they
do have implications for the finished product. Polysaccharides contribute to the body of a coffee and
behave curiously, especially mannopyranose. All other polysaccharides peak around the 4 minute mark
and then fall slowly as the roast finishes, except mannopyranose, which continues to climb strongly
throughout the roasting process.

Graph H – Myoinositol, choline, 2-furylmethanol, y-butyrolactone, 5-hydroxymethylfurfural

Myoinositol and choline only tend to faintly decrease throughout the roasting process.

2-furylmethanol has no to low presence in green, unroasted coffee and peaks here at the 6 minute
mark, then flatlines until the end of the roasting process. 2-furylmethanol has gotten mixed reviews on
its pleasantness factor in roasted coffee, not surprisingly, as with many of these compounds it greatly
depends on how much of its concentration is present.

Gamma Butyrolactone, besides being used for commercial purposes as a superglue remover, Flament
writes, “This widely distributed and common aroma constituent has a faint, sweet-aromatic odor
(Arctander, 1967).” It also peaks around the 6 to 6 and a half minute mark and continues steady
throughout the roasting process.

5-Hydroxymethylfurfural is generated in food by the heating process. About this compound Flament
writes “The odor is warm-herbaceous, winey-ethereal, remotely resembling that of Hungarian
chamomile (matricaria oil). The flavor is sweet, herbaceous, hay-like, mildly tobacco-like (Arctander,
1967). The flavor threshold is 100 ppm in water as determined by Brule et al. (1971) who found that it
gave a mouldy, bitter taste depending on the concentration.”

The Takeaway

The underlying chemical roasting and cupping correlates continue to be drawn thicker everyday. While research like this is being done in chemistry laboratories, similar research is being done by roasters and cuppers in the specialty coffee world. At some point in time these two worlds should fuse together in a more robust way. It is to that end that roasters and cuppers can use this data to enhance their coffee experience and assist them in going backward from the cupping table to the roaster to try and optimize for the presence of certain components. While there are many answers in this study, there are also lots of questions that remain unanswered.

Questions like:

What happens with coffee chemistry in a drum roaster that is different than a fluid
bed roaster? Or what if we change the roast curve just a little and try to optimize for particular
compounds? Or what is the deal with mannypyranose and why does it behave different than the other
polysaccharides? Through research like this study, we are learning a lot about the individual semi
volatile and volatile organic compounds that make coffee so unique. However, it may take many more
years before we fully grasp the benefit of this data at the farmer, roaster and barista level. That is
where the rubber hits the road and we need roaster scientists to do some of the heavy lifting with the
data that we have available.
